## General Slide Design Rules

These rules apply to every deck, regardless of visual style. The style block that follows defines *how* things look; these rules define *what* a good slide is.

### The "earn your place" principle

Every element on a slide must serve the message. If you can remove something and the slide still reads, remove it.

- **No filler.** No icons-for-decoration, no "visual interest" shapes, no padded stat grids where you invented the third metric to match the layout. One thousand no's for every yes.
- **No lorem / placeholder copy.** Pull real content from sources or leave space.
- **No "data slop."** Fake percentages, generic icon sets, meaningless sparklines — all forbidden.
- **Ask the content, not the template.** The right format for a quote is not the right format for a comparison. Let the content pick the layout.

### Commit to a system up front

Before writing slide 1:

- Pick **one** background color for content slides (a second is allowed only for section dividers).
- Pick **at most two** type families (display + body). Declare them as `--font-display` and `--font-body`.
- Pick a **palette of 4–6 colors max**: one dominant, one secondary, one accent, plus 1–2 neutrals. Use the 60/30/10 weighting.
- Pick a **type scale** and **spacing scale** as CSS custom properties. Reuse them everywhere.

Consistency across slides is what separates "professional deck" from "ten random slides."

### Layout Variety & Rhythm

Never repeat the same archetype on consecutive slides. A good deck alternates dense/breathing, text/visual, wide/focused.

A 10-slide deck should include a mix of: hero cover, section divider, hero-stat, data chart, KPI grid, framework/matrix/timeline, quote/callout, comparison, closing. Never a whole deck of "title + three bullets."

### Visual Hierarchy

- Every slide has **exactly one focal point** — the element the eye hits first.
- Use dramatic size contrast: titles are 3–5× body, hero stats 8–12×.
- Apply 60/30/10 color weighting. Never give all colors equal weight.
- Key numbers are displayed **large**, with a tiny label beneath — not buried in prose.

### Typography Discipline

- Titles are **insights**: "Enterprise ACV grew 38%" — never topic labels like "Revenue".
- `text-wrap: balance` on titles; `text-wrap: pretty` on body paragraphs.
- Left-align body and lists. Center only titles, hero stats, and cover text.
- Generous line-height: 1.1–1.2 on display, 1.4–1.6 on body.
- Numbers: `font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums` everywhere digits align.
- Minimum readable size on this 1920×1080 canvas: **24px**. Never smaller.

### Title length

A slide title is a **headline, not a paragraph**. Treat it like a newspaper kicker: one short sentence the audience can absorb in one glance.

- **Target ≤ 60 characters, hard cap 80.** This is a hard cap — not "around 80".
- **Never wrap past 2 lines.** If `text-wrap: balance` produces 3 lines, the title is too long; rewrite, don't shrink the font.
- **Push detail into the subtitle.** The subtitle (32–44px) carries qualifiers, dates, source notes, scoping — not the title.

Good (≤ 60 chars, 1 line):
- "Enterprise ACV grew 38% as mid-market stalled"
- "Why our EMEA retention strategy is breaking"
- "Three reasons we're delaying the launch"

Bad (over cap / wraps to 3+ lines):
- "An Overview of How Our Enterprise Customer Acquisition Strategy Has Performed Throughout Fiscal Year 2024" — topic label dressed up as a sentence
- "Enterprise ACV grew 38% in FY24 driven by upsell into existing logos in North America, while mid-market new-logo bookings stalled due to extended sales cycles" — qualifiers belong in the subtitle

### Every Slide Earns its Visual

No text-only slides. Every slide includes at least one of: a chart, an image, a hero number, a framework diagram, a quote treated as a visual element, or a dramatic typographic composition.

### Spacing & Margins

- Outer margin: **minimum 80px**; prefer 96–160px.
- Use the 8-based spacing scale: `4, 8, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128, 160`.
- Never crowd to the pixel edge. Whitespace is the design.

### AVOID (AI-slop checklist)

These are the tells of a machine-generated deck. Every one is forbidden unless the style explicitly calls for it:

- Rounded rectangles with a colored left border as callout boxes
- Gradient backgrounds on every slide (use them sparingly, as accent only)
- Emoji as icons or decoration
- Drop shadows on everything; excessive depth / glassmorphism
- Cute hand-drawn-style SVG illustrations (unless the style is Whiteboard)
- Decorative accent lines or underlines beneath every title
- Three-bullet lists as the default for everything
- Low-contrast text (light on light, dark on dark)
- Centered body text or centered bulleted lists
- Content crammed edge-to-edge with no margin
- Equal density across every slide (no rhythm)
- Generic topic-label titles ("Overview," "Agenda," "Conclusion")
- Title that wraps to 3+ lines or reads like an abstract instead of a headline
- Made-up statistics or fabricated quotes
- Styling one slide beautifully and leaving the rest inconsistent
